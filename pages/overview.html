Burdens of Home Energy Costs

Understanding how energy costs impact household budgets, create financial hardship for millions of American families, and contribute to climate-warming greenhouse gas emissions.

Energy costs touch every household, but the burden is not shared equally. In 2022, American households collectively spent approximately $285 billion on energy to power, heat, and cool their homes — with low-income families bearing a disproportionate share of this burden.

01 • Costs for Energy at Home

What households pay to stay powered

In 2022, American households collectively spent billions of dollars on energy to power, heat, and cool their homes.

  • Total spending: approximately $285 billion for electricity and heating fuels
  • Low-income households (earning less than 80% of area median income) paid $120 billion to meet their energy needs
  • This represents 42% of total residential energy spending
02 • Household Budget Impacts

When bills squeeze household budgets

Energy costs create significant financial burdens for low-income families. Energy burden is defined as the percentage of household income spent on energy costs.

  • 67% of low-income households spend more than 6% of their gross income on energy (ACEEE, 2020)
  • This exceeds the affordability threshold recommended by energy assistance programs
  • Households have less money for other expenses, often forgoing medical care or other important necessities
  • Annual gap: $30 billion spent beyond the 6% threshold across all households
  • This amount represents how much can be saved through bill assistance, energy efficiency, and improved technology
Understanding Energy Burden: A household is considered "energy burdened" when energy costs exceed 6% of income. Low-income households, rural households, households of color, and renters tend to have higher energy cost burdens, driven less by energy use and more by older, inefficient appliances, poorly-insulated homes, and expensive heating fuels.
03 • Residential Greenhouse Gas Emissions

Household energy and climate

Residential energy use is a major contributor to U.S. greenhouse gas emissions. Cutting bills and cutting carbon often move in the same direction.

  • Total emissions: 303 million metric tons of CO₂ equivalent from home energy consumption in 2024 (US Energy Information Administration)
  • This represents 6% of direct U.S. emissions
  • Energy use from low- to moderate-income households contributes 40% of home energy consumption
  • Major opportunities exist to cut emissions while alleviating high bill burdens
04 • Why This Matters

Affordability and equity go together

Energy affordability is both a social equity and climate issue. The right data helps direct resources where they do the most good.

  • Health & Safety: Households struggling with bills face increased risks from inadequate heating, cooling, and unsafe energy practices (like using the oven for heating)
  • Upfront Costs: Low-income families cannot afford efficiency upgrades or electrification that would reduce bills and emissions
  • Program Gaps: Weatherization, efficiency, and assistance programs exist but lack detailed local data to target investments effectively
  • Energy Equity: Equitable solutions require understanding energy burdens across different demographics and geographies
